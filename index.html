What is QRISK?

QRISK is an estimate of how likely you are to have a heart attack or stroke in the next 10 years. It considers multiple factors including age, cholesterol, blood pressure, and other health markers.

Important: The cholesterol value itself is not that important â€“ it's the combination of cholesterol and many other factors that determine your risk.

NICE (the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) generally advises cholesterol-lowering medication (atorvastatin 20mg) if your QRISK is above 10%.

Do I "need" to take a statin?

Not everyone who takes a statin will benefit from it, so it's a personal choice based on your preferences, weighing potential benefits and risks according to your QRISK score.

Understanding the numbers (10% risk example)

If 100 people with a 10% baseline risk take a statin for 10 years, 4 will avoid a cardiovascular event compared with if they hadn't taken a statin.

The higher your baseline risk, the greater the potential benefit. At 15% risk, 6 people out of 100 will avoid an event. At 20% risk, 8 people will benefit.

ðŸ’Š About Atorvastatin

Atorvastatin is a medication that lowers cholesterol and, more importantly, reduces your risk of heart attacks and strokes. The higher your QRISK, the more benefit you'll get from taking a statin.

Muscle Ache â€“ Common but probably not the statin!

Statins have had some bad press regarding muscle aches, but the evidence tells a different story.

The ASCOT trial with over 10,000 people found:

  • 2.03% of those taking atorvastatin developed muscular pains
  • 2.00% of those taking placebo developed muscular pains

Result: No statistically significant link between statins and muscular pain.

Liver Changes

About 3% of people may experience a rise in liver enzymes on blood tests. This is generally not harmful and may be linked to removing fat from the liver (a good thing!).

Only 1 in 1,100 people have an ongoing rise in liver tests, and about 1 in 100-200 people have to stop due to liver problems (rarely serious).

Diabetes Risk

Statins can increase blood sugars and the risk of type 2 diabetes, but this generally only affects people who already have raised sugars (pre-diabetes).

Important: Even for people with diabetes, we still recommend statins because the benefits of preventing heart attacks and strokes outweigh the risks.

What if I get side effects?

If you experience side effects, stop the statin and the symptoms are likely to resolve.

Tip: If you get minor symptoms (tummy ache, muscle pains), they may not be related to the statin. Try this:

  1. Stop the statin for one month
  2. Restart it
  3. If symptoms recur, it's likely a true side effect

If unsure, please contact us for a review.

  • 12 months after starting atorvastatin

It's worth checking your cholesterol (full lipid profile) once after starting treatment. Beyond that, there's no benefit in monitoring your cholesterol or QRISK further.
